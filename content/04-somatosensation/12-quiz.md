title: Module quiz — Somatosensation
lede: Seven questions on the module whose main job is a correction — that a mapped, metric, topographic system can still put its most important information in spike timing. Every distractor is right under a different assumption, so read the feedback on the ones you get right too.
estimatedMinutes: 24
---

This quiz tests whether you can *use* touch, not whether you remember it. Touch is in this course as a control condition: it has everything vision's ordering was supposed to buy, and several of its most important results do not depend on that ordering at all. Most of the questions ask you to carry a tactile result into a modality with no ordering at all.

Answer before revealing. The distractor feedback is where most of the teaching is.

<x-callout class="x-callout is-key">
<div class="x-callout-title">What to carry into the next module</div>
Touch hands forward four things, and the first is a correction rather than a tool.

<strong>A map does not make the code spatial</strong> (§4.7) — a topographic, metric, mapped system carrying fine texture in millisecond timing. Applied to olfaction, it dissolves the exclusivity of the "which glomeruli versus when" dispute.

<strong>Phase-referenced decoding</strong> (§4.9) — whisking and sniffing share frequency band, voluntary control, a phase reference, and the property of altering the stimulus. Barrel cortex's decoder is worked out; the bulb's is not, and the transfer is blocked on what the phase encodes rather than on the machinery.

<strong>A second thalamus-free pathway</strong> (§4.6) — spinocerebellar routes bypass the relay under a tight deadline for a consumer that wants speed. Evidence for the computational over the antiquity account of olfaction's missing relay.

<strong>Free computation from accessory structures</strong> (§4.11) — Pacinian lamellae, cochlear gradients, canal inertia. Olfaction pays neurally for all of it, so cross-modal complexity comparisons need to net out the physics.
</x-callout>
