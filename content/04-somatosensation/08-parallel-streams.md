## What and where, again

Beyond S1, tactile processing diverges toward **secondary somatosensory cortex** for object identity and texture, and toward **posterior parietal cortex** for spatial and action-related processing.

That is now three modalities — vision (§1.8), audition (§2.8), touch — arriving independently at a division between identity and location. The invariance argument holds in each: recognising an object requires invariance to where it touched you; guiding a hand requires invariance to what it is.

**Three independent instances make this about as well-supported as comparative claims in this course get.** And the prediction for olfaction correspondingly strengthens: §3.8 argued that identity and location are *more* dissimilar in olfaction than in any of these, and nobody can point confidently to the second stream.

## Discriminative versus affective touch

Touch adds a second division that the others do not have, and it is the one that connects to gustation.

The **discriminative** system — dorsal columns, VPL, S1 — is optimised for spatial and temporal detail. The **affective** system — spinothalamic, running to insular cortex and limbic targets — carries pain, temperature, and pleasant touch, with poor spatial resolution and strong valence.

<x-callout class="x-callout is-key">
<div class="x-callout-title">Two divisions, two different arguments</div>
It is worth being precise that these two splits are justified differently, because the distinction transfers.

<strong>What/where is an invariance argument.</strong> Identity needs invariance to position; position needs invariance to identity. No single representation provides both.

<strong>Discriminative/affective is a loss-function argument.</strong> Fine texture judgement wants metric detail preserved. Pain wants a fast, categorical, high-priority signal that drives withdrawal — and preserving metric detail about exactly how much it hurts is worth little.

§1.1 introduced the loss-function argument using gustation; §5 develops it. Touch is where both arguments operate <em>in the same modality at once</em>, which makes it the clearest demonstration that they are distinct principles rather than one principle described two ways.

<strong>For olfaction:</strong> §3.8 identified the piriform/cortical-amygdala split as a loss-function division — learned identity against innate valence. The what/where division is the invariance one, and it is separately predicted and separately missing.
