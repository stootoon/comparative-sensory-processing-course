Proprioceptive information takes two broadly separate routes.

**The conscious route:** dorsal column–medial lemniscal pathway → VPL thalamus → areas 3a and 2 of somatosensory cortex. Conventional, four synapses, obligatory relay.

**The cerebellar route:** spinocerebellar tracts → cerebellum, **bypassing the thalamus entirely.**

## The second bypass

§4.6 introduced this and it is worth stating fully here, because it is the strongest evidence available against the antiquity explanation of olfaction's missing relay.

<x-callout class="x-callout is-key">
<div class="x-callout-title">What the two thalamus-skipping pathways have in common</div>
<strong>Not antiquity.</strong> Proprioception is not especially ancient, and the spinocerebellar pathways are not evolutionary relics.

<strong>What they share with olfaction:</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>A tight deadline.</strong> Cerebellar forward models must update within the motor control loop; olfaction must decide within a sniff.</li>
<li><strong>A consumer wanting speed over re-mapping.</strong> The cerebellum wants current limb state, not an attentionally-gated re-representation. Piriform wants an odour identity to associate.</li>
<li><strong>Nothing geometric to re-map.</strong> §1.6 argued thalamic relays perform gating and routing that presuppose something worth routing. Neither the cerebellar nor the olfactory consumer is asking for it.</li>
</ul>

<strong>The conclusion:</strong> thalamic relays are not a universal requirement for reaching central structures. They appear where a signal must be gated, attentionally modulated, and routed between cortical areas. Where the consumer wants a fast, direct, unmodulated estimate, the relay is skipped — in a modality of any evolutionary age.

This substantially weakens the "olfaction is old" explanation and strengthens the computational one (§3.6).
</x-callout>

## Note on areas 3a and 2

The cortical proprioceptive representation is real but thin. Area 3a receives muscle spindle input, area 2 combines cutaneous and proprioceptive signals, and both are small compared with the tactile areas 3b and 1.

§7.7 takes up what that thinness means.
